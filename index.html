City Seal


The city seal is used for all items related to the Gainesville City Commission. It is also used to mark all items of a permanent and lasting nature. The Clerk of the Commission serves as custodian of the city seal. Used for:

  • City flag
  • City Hall auditorium signage
  • Legal signage
  • City Commission ordinances
  • City Commission and Mayoral proclamations
  • City Commission and Clerk of the City Commission letterhead, business cards (if requested)
  • City Commission appointed boards and committees letterhead when sending external correspondence via official City Commission action
  • City Commission reports and plans
  • City Commission agenda and minutes
  • City Commission adopted documents
  • City board-appointed commissions, task forces
  • City Commission official and/or ceremonial items (pins, pens, plaques)
  • Permanent building signage
  • Employee service pins, service awards
  • Benefit forms and statements
  • City citations
  • Legal documents
  • Legal signage
  • Building capstones, historical markers

Digital Color Palette

The Digital Color palette is slightly different than the City of Gainesville official brand colors and the Department of Doing brand colors. We have migrated the official colors to similar colors in the USWDS2.0 color system token chart. Each color now has a number associated to it. These numbers will help you attain proper contrast ratios for accessibility and ADA compliance. Please read about the USWDS2.0 Magic Number below for more information.

USWDS2.0 Magic Number

Always use the USWDS2.0 color equivalent of a brand color to ensure appropriate contrast ratios. Link for more information.

We call the difference in grade between any two colors the magic number. Magic numbers have important contrast implications:

      • A magic number of 40+ results in WCAG 2.0 AA Large Text contrast (example: gray-90 and indigo-warm-50v).
      • A magic number of 50+ results in WCAG 2.0 AA contrast or AAA Large Text contrast (example: gray-90 and red-40).
      • A magic number of 70+ results in WCAG 2.0 AAA contrast (example: gray-10 and red-80).
      • Colors of grade 50 result in Section 508 AA contrast against both pure white (grade 0) and pure black (grade 100).

Use USWDS magic numbers to choose accessible color combinations from any palette and color family.

Magic Number Example

Here is an example of how to use it:

Our primary brand color is teal. The USWDS2.0 name for that color is: "mint-cool-30v"

      • Let's imagine you want to use that as the background for a block of text.
      • We know that we want a "Magic Number" to be 70 or above.
      • If we use white font (score of 0) on the teal (score of 30), then the difference between 0 and 30 is 30. 30 does not pass any WCAG accessibility standards.
      • If we use black font (score of 100) on the teal (score of 30), then the difference between 30 and 100 is 70. 70 passes all levels of WCAG accessibility standards.
      • You should use black font on the teal background (even if you like how the white font looks better).

City of Gainesville Editorial Guidelines



Editorial Guidelines


All written content by the City should meet the following three guidelines:

  • Correct font for what the text will be read in (print, digital, UI).
  • Appropriate contrast ratios (text and background) to pass WCAG AAA (accessibility) standards.
  • Follow Plain Language Act to ensure text is understandable for the audience.

Do This...

  • Write for 6th grade reading level. Use Hemmingway app, Readable.io, or MS Word Readability Statistics
  • Use short sentences (less than 20 syllables)
  • Use short words (less than 4 syllables, less than 12 letters)
  • Use active voice
  • Left align sentences and paragraphs
  • Use Serif fonts for print
  • Use Sans Serif fonts for digital

Don't Do This...

  • USE ALL CAPS IN TITLES
  • Center or right align multiple rows of text
  • Full justify multiple rows of text
  • Hyphenate words across rows
  • Allow table rows to break across pages without duplicating table headers for each page
  • Use symbols in sentences - A/B, A+B, A&B

WHY CAN'T I USE ALL CAPS?

It’s a usability / accessibility issue. Americans read words based on word shape, not actual letters in the word. In the picture below, you can see that the left word shape is one big box and could be any word. The shape on the right has ascenders and descenders which make it more instantly recognizable.
If you blur out your eyes like in the middle picture, you can probably still read the top paragraph but the bottom paragraph is a lot harder to read. When you do eye-tracking studies and time-on-task measurements, all caps performs significantly worse with more user errors. Also, it’s kinda rude to write in all caps.






Fonts (Print, Digital, UI)

The City of Gainesville uses different fonts depending on the medium (print, digital, or UI).

Fonts for Print

The City of Gainesville has 2 fonts that can be used in special branded print materials. They are Publico and National. Please refer to Wordmark Styleguide for how to use in conjunction with the Wordmark. Here is a quick overview of what the fonts look like.

If you don't have those specialty fonts, you can use the following free fonts that should be installed on your machine.

Use Serif fonts for printed materials. "Serif" fonts have extra lines and swirls on characters. These make words easier to read for long printed materials like newspapers, reports, and flyers - printed pages that you hold in your hand and read.


Recommended Serif Fonts (Print):
Use these for text that is displayed in print (magazines, newspapers, flyers, books).



Fonts for Digital

If you are making something to be seen digitally (like a PowerPoint Report), use Sans Serif fonts for digital displays. "Sans Serif" means fonts without the extra swirlywoos. They are generally simpler and don't have extra lines on letters and numbers. Sans Serif fonts are easier to read on screens.

Font options: Roboto, Arial, Calibri, Tahoma, Verdana

Fonts for UI

If you are creating new digital applications, we recommend a Sans Serif font. The following fonts are available for free and your developers will be able to reference their web servers easily.

  • Google Noto is the recommended font if you need to translate your application into multiple languages. It will provide the most consistent visual display in all languages.
  • USWDS2.0 Public Sans is the recommended font if you are following USWDS2.0 design standards and are collaborating with Federal agencies.
  • Google Open Sans or Google Roboto are recommended if you are using a third party application and are given an option for what font to use.


Contrast Ratios



Always check contrast ratios to make sure your writing is legible. You will need to know what color you are using for your letters and what color the background is. Be extremely careful when using letters on photos. You must ensure that all colors in the background photo meet accessibility requirements with your letter color.

Use USWDS Color tokens: https://v2.designsystem.digital.gov/design-tokens/color/system-tokens/

Take a look at the Digital Color Palette section in the "Colors" section for help with USWDS Magic Number.

Contrast Ratio Checker: https://webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker/

Web browser color picker: https://www.colorzilla.com/





Plain Language



The City of Gainesville follows the Plain Language Act of 2010. The law requires federal agencies use clear language that the public can understand.

Plain Language Law - https://www.plainlanguage.gov/

All content should go through an editorial review app to make sure that we are speaking in plain language.

Readable.io - https://readable.com/

Hemmingway App - http://www.hemingwayapp.com/
